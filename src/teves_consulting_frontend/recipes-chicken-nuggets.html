Whole Foods Recipes: Homemade Chicken Nuggets

Last updated: March 2026

A whole-food version of homemade chicken nuggets using real chicken, flour, eggs, breadcrumbs, and cast iron cooking. This first version focuses on simple ingredients, familiar flavor, and a cleaner alternative to heavily processed frozen nuggets.

Key takeaways
  • Use whole chicken pieces: chunked chicken gives a more natural texture than blended chicken paste.
  • Cast iron works very well: medium heat and enough oil create a crisp coating without deep frying.
  • Homemade breadcrumbs help: real bread creates better flavor and texture than industrial coatings.
  • Uniform size matters: similar nugget size helps them cook evenly.

Purpose

This recipe replaces one of the most processed foods in the standard family meal rotation with a simple whole-food version. The goal is to create a kid-friendly chicken nugget that still feels familiar while improving ingredient quality, oil quality, and overall transparency.

Total time

  • Prep time: about 20 minutes
  • Cook time: about 15–20 minutes
  • Total: about 35–40 minutes

Ingredients

  • 1.5–2 lbs chicken breast or thighs
  • 1 cup flour
  • 2 eggs
  • 1.5 cups breadcrumbs (preferably homemade — see section below)
  • Salt
  • Optional: garlic powder, paprika, black pepper
  • Avocado oil for pan frying

Breadcrumbs (Homemade — Recommended)

For best results, use homemade breadcrumbs instead of store-bought versions. This keeps the recipe fully within a whole-food framework and improves both flavor and texture.

If you already have homemade bread, this becomes a simple extension of your existing system. See the homemade bread recipe for the base.

Method

  • Use bread that is at least 2 days old
  • Cut into slices
  • Toast until fully dry and lightly crisp
  • Blend in a blender or food processor until crumb texture forms

Storage

  • Store in an airtight container in the refrigerator
  • Use within up to 7 days

Why this matters

  • Avoids preservatives and low-quality oils in packaged breadcrumbs
  • Creates better texture and flavor
  • Maintains full control over ingredients

Equipment

  • 10-inch cast iron skillet
  • Knife and cutting board
  • Three bowls for breading
  • Tongs or spatula

Preparation

Cut the chicken into small nugget-sized pieces. Try to keep the size fairly even so they cook at the same speed. Set up three bowls for coating: flour in the first bowl, beaten eggs in the second, and breadcrumbs in the third.

Method

  1. Season the flour lightly with salt and optional garlic powder or paprika.
  2. Beat the eggs until smooth in the second bowl.
  3. Place breadcrumbs in the third bowl.
  4. Coat each piece of chicken first in flour, then egg, then breadcrumbs.
  5. Press the breadcrumbs lightly onto the chicken so the coating adheres well.
  6. Heat cast iron over medium heat and add enough avocado oil to lightly coat the bottom of the pan.
  7. Place nuggets into the pan without overcrowding. Cook in batches if needed.
  8. Cook about 3–4 minutes per side, turning only when the coating is golden.
  9. Continue cooking until the chicken is fully cooked through and the outside is crisp.
  10. Transfer to a plate or rack and let them rest briefly before serving.

Finish

Serve hot. These work well as a family meal, a kid-friendly lunch, or part of a larger plate with potatoes, bread, fruit, or simple vegetables.

Notes

  • Do not blend the chicken: blending creates a processed texture closer to fast-food nuggets.
  • Use medium heat: high heat can brown the coating before the inside finishes cooking.
  • Do not overcrowd the pan: spacing helps the nuggets crisp properly.
  • Breadcrumb texture matters: slightly coarse breadcrumbs usually create the best crust.
  • Homemade breadcrumbs recommended: using your own bread significantly improves quality and keeps the recipe fully whole-food.

Personal note

This recipe is meant to replace one of the most common ultra-processed convenience foods with something made from recognizable ingredients. It fits the broader goal of building a home food system where even the most familiar kid foods can be made with better quality and more control.
