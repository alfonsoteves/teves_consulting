Whole Foods Recipes: Rice Pudding

Last updated: May 2026

Rice pudding is one of the simplest comfort foods: inexpensive ingredients, gentle flavor, and a surprisingly filling result built from pantry basics.

Key takeaways
  • Simple pantry ingredients — rice, milk, cinnamon, vanilla, and a little sweetness.
  • Low heat matters — patience creates creaminess and prevents scorching.
  • Texture is adjustable — thicker or looser depending on preference.
  • Works warm or cold — flexible for dessert, breakfast, or a simple comfort snack.

Purpose

Build a simple, repeatable comfort food using inexpensive ingredients and low-complexity cooking.

This recipe focuses on household practicality: minimal ingredients, forgiving execution, and a texture that can be adjusted over time without needing precise technique.

Total time

About 45–60 minutes, mostly gentle simmering. Active time is low, but occasional stirring improves texture and prevents sticking.

Ingredients (4–6 servings)

  • Cooked white rice — 2 cups
  • Whole milk — 3–4 cups
  • Sugar or honey — ¼ to ⅓ cup
  • Vanilla extract — 1 tsp
  • Cinnamon — to taste
  • Salt — small pinch
  • Butter — 1 tbsp optional

Equipment

  • Medium pot or saucepan
  • Wooden spoon or silicone spatula
  • Measuring cups and spoons
  • Storage container for leftovers

Method

1) Combine ingredients

Add cooked rice, milk, sugar or honey, salt, and optional butter to a pot over medium-low heat.

2) Simmer gently

Cook slowly while stirring occasionally. Lower heat is more important than speed; rice pudding thickens gradually as the rice releases starch into the milk.

If the mixture thickens too much, add more milk in small amounts until the texture feels right.

3) Finish and adjust

Once creamy, remove from heat and stir in vanilla and cinnamon. Taste and adjust sweetness if needed.

4) Rest briefly

Rice pudding thickens further as it cools. Let it rest several minutes before serving warm, or refrigerate for a colder and firmer texture.

Texture control

Rice pudding can be adjusted easily depending on preference:

  • Thicker: simmer longer or use less milk.
  • Creamier: add milk gradually during cooking.
  • Softer rice: cook longer on lower heat.
  • Less sweet: reduce sugar and let cinnamon or vanilla carry more flavor.

Practical takeaway: Rice pudding is forgiving. Small adjustments during cooking matter more than exact measurements.

Simple vs Refined Method

This recipe can be run in two ways depending on your goal: easy comfort food or more controlled texture.

Simple method

Use leftover cooked rice, milk, sweetener, and cinnamon. Simmer gently until creamy. This is the best version for reducing waste and turning basic food into something satisfying.

Refined method

For more control, start with rice cooked slightly firm and finish it slowly in milk. Add milk gradually and stir more often near the end. This produces a smoother, creamier pudding with more consistent texture.

Troubleshooting

  • Too thick: add milk a little at a time and stir over low heat.
  • Too thin: simmer longer, stirring occasionally, until it reduces.
  • Sticking or scorching: lower the heat and stir more often near the bottom of the pot.
  • Not creamy enough: cook longer on low heat so the rice releases more starch.
  • Too sweet: add more rice or milk and rebalance with cinnamon and a tiny pinch of salt.

Storage and serving

Store refrigerated for several days in a sealed container. Rice pudding thickens significantly when cold; add a small amount of milk when reheating if you want a looser texture.

Works well as dessert, breakfast, or a simple comfort snack. Serve warm with cinnamon, or cold from the refrigerator when you want something easy and filling.

Family reaction

Rice pudding feels old-fashioned in the best way: inexpensive, calming, and surprisingly satisfying from very simple ingredients. It also works well as a low-friction comfort food when the household wants something warm and familiar without a complicated recipe.
