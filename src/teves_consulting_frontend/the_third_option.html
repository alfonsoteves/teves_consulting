Thinking Better · Essay Two

The Third Option

On forced polarization, intellectual independence, and better questions.

“Sometimes the most important choice is refusing the frame you were given.”

First Edition · Teves Consulting · 2026
Thinking Better is a three-part essay series about engineering judgment, intellectual independence, and the role of memory in helping people ask better questions.

A false choice rarely announces itself as one.

It usually arrives disguised as clarity: choose this or that, support one side or the other, decide now.

The structure feels natural because the human mind is comfortable with categories. Two options reduce complexity. They save time. They create the reassuring feeling that the problem has been understood.

But sometimes the choice is not describing reality. It is constructing a cage around it.

Forced Polarization

Take a complicated issue. Remove the nuance. Reduce it to two positions. Then insist that everyone select one.

The technique works because resistance is tiring. Holding uncertainty in mind requires energy. Choosing a side feels like resolution, even when the frame itself is incomplete.

Once a position becomes part of identity, changing it becomes harder. People begin defending the team rather than examining the evidence.

The Question Before the Answer

Building AI systems has reinforced a lesson that applies far beyond technology: the question is often more important than the answer.

Ask whether something is simply good or bad and the conclusion has already been narrowed. A better inquiry might be:

What is this actually for?
Who benefits?
What am I not being shown?

Binary choices sometimes reflect reality. More often than we admit, however, the binary was manufactured before we arrived.

The Third Option

The third option is not indecision and it is not compromise for its own sake. It is the refusal to accept a frame merely because it was presented confidently.

The third option begins with the possibility that both available answers are responding to the wrong question.

Sometimes it sounds like: “Both framings are missing something.” Sometimes: “This is not a yes-or-no question.” Sometimes it is simply: “I do not have to decide today.”

That final response matters because forced polarization often depends on urgency. Pick now. React now. Join now. A pause interrupts the machinery.

Not Neutrality

Looking for a third option does not mean refusing every position. Some choices are genuinely binary. Sometimes one side is supported by the evidence and the other is not.

The goal is not permanent neutrality. The goal is noticing when the available positions have been designed to prevent independent thought.

The skill is not avoiding every side. It is recognizing when you are being herded into one.

One Second of Resistance

The next time a headline, argument, advertisement, or political message demands an immediate allegiance, pause for one extra second.

Who built this choice—and what did they leave out?

That pause may not produce an instant answer. It may produce something more valuable: enough distance to ask a better question.

Not choosing a side faster.
Asking a better question slower.
