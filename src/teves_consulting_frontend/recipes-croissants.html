Whole Foods Recipes: Croissants (Version 1)

Last updated: March 2026

A first practical version of classic butter croissants using real butter, a slow three-day structure, and a home-kitchen approach to lamination.

Key takeaways
  • Version 1 is about learning — not perfect bakery results.
  • Three-day process — dough, lamination, shaping, and baking are easier when separated.
  • Butter temperature matters — cold but flexible wins.
  • Patience improves results — rushing usually produces dense croissants.

Purpose

A first practical version of classic butter croissants built for home kitchens. The goal is not bakery perfection on the first run. The goal is to learn the process, build confidence with lamination, and produce a repeatable version using real butter and simple ingredients.

Ingredients

  • 500 g flour
  • 10 g salt
  • 50 g sugar
  • 10 g active dry yeast (about 1 packet)
  • 300 ml milk
  • 40 g softened butter (for the dough)
  • 250 g cold butter (for the butter block)
  • 1 egg (for egg wash)

Equipment

  • Rolling pin
  • Parchment paper
  • Mixing bowl
  • Baking tray
  • Refrigerator space for resting dough

Three-day structure

Day 1 — Dough + butter block

  • Mix flour, salt, sugar, yeast, milk, and softened butter into a soft dough.
  • Knead lightly until smooth.
  • Cover and refrigerate overnight.
  • Separately, shape the cold butter into a flat rectangle between parchment paper and refrigerate.

Day 2 — Lamination

  • Roll dough into a rectangle and enclose the butter block.
  • Roll gently and perform the first fold.
  • Refrigerate 30–45 minutes.
  • Repeat for a total of 3 folds, chilling between each round.

Day 3 — Shape + bake

  • Roll dough thin.
  • Cut triangles and shape croissants.
  • Proof until puffy.
  • Brush with egg wash.
  • Bake until golden brown.

Method notes

  • Butter temperature matters: cold but flexible is ideal.
  • If butter cracks: it is too cold.
  • If butter smears: it is too warm. Refrigerate and continue later.
  • Do not rush proofing: under-proofed croissants turn dense and bread-like.
  • Resting is part of the recipe: slow process beats rushed process.

Expected result for Version 1

The first batch is meant to teach the process more than deliver perfect bakery lamination. Even an imperfect batch should still produce buttery, flaky pastries with clear layers and a much better ingredient profile than most commercial croissants.

Why this recipe belongs here

Croissants are a skill-building recipe. They move beyond simple repetition and introduce lamination, patience, and dough control. They also fit the larger whole-food cooking philosophy: real flour, real butter, real time, and no additives.

Personal note

This version is intentionally simple and realistic. It is a foundation for learning. Later versions can improve shaping, proofing, and lamination precision after the first real batch is completed.
